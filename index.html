Landslide

Landslides, also known as landslips or rockslides, are geological phenomena involving a wide range of ground movements, such as rockfalls, mudflows, shallow or deep-seated slope failures, and debris flows. Landslides occur in various environments characterized by steep or gentle slope gradients, from mountain ranges to coastal cliffs and even underwater, where they are called submarine landslides.

Gravity is the primary driving force for a landslide, but other factors, such as the stability of the slope material, can also contribute. In many cases, landslides are triggered by specific events like heavy rainfall, earthquakes, or human activities such as road construction. Landslides are often worsened by human development, urban sprawl, resource exploitation, and land degradation, all of which reduce soil stability. Additionally, climate change and global warming have increased the frequency of extreme weather events that can trigger landslides.

Causes of Landslides

Landslides occur when the slope undergoes processes that change its condition from stable to unstable. This change is usually due to a decrease in the shear strength of the slope material, an increase in the shear stress on the material, or a combination of both. Factors that can lead to a landslide include:

  • Increased water content (loss of suction) or saturation due to rainwater infiltration, snowmelt, or glacier melt.
  • Rising groundwater levels or increased pore water pressure.
  • Hydrostatic pressure in cracks and fractures.
  • Loss of vegetative structure, soil nutrients, and soil stability, especially after wildfires.
  • Erosion of the slope by rivers or sea waves.
  • Physical and chemical weathering caused by freezing and thawing, heating and cooling, or mineral dissolution.
  • Ground shaking from earthquakes, which can destabilize slopes directly or weaken the material over time.
  • Changes in pore fluid composition or temperature.

Wayanad Landslide

The 2024 Wayanad landslides occurred in the Punjirimattom, Mundakkai, Chooralmala, Attamala, Meppadi, and Kunhome villages of Wayanad district, Kerala, India, on 30 July 2024. Heavy rains triggered the collapse of hillsides, sending torrents of mud, water, and boulders cascading down onto the area. The landslides resulted in at least 429 deaths, over 378 injuries, and more than 130 people missing, marking one of the deadliest natural disasters in Kerala's history. These events are part of a pattern of extreme weather in India in recent years.

Flood

Flooding is a temporary overflow of water onto land that is normally dry. Floods can result from various sources, including heavy rainfall, snowmelt, coastal storms, storm surges, river overflows, and dam failures. Floods can be deadly, especially when people do not evacuate in time or attempt to cross flooded areas.

Flood Control

Flood control methods, such as levees, reservoirs, and floodwalls, are used to reduce or prevent the detrimental effects of floodwaters. These methods can be structural or non-structural and are essential for managing floods and minimizing their impact on communities. Effective flood management also includes land-use planning, early warning systems, and flood insurance.

Types of Flood

  • Areal flooding: Occurs on flat or low-lying areas when water accumulates more rapidly than it can infiltrate or run off.
  • River flooding: Happens when rivers overflow their banks, often due to heavy rain or snowmelt.
  • Coastal flooding: Caused by storm surges, high tides, or tsunamis in coastal areas.
  • Urban flooding: Results from overwhelmed drainage systems in cities, often caused by heavy rain or storm surges.
  • Intentional floods: Occur when land is deliberately flooded for agricultural, military, or river management purposes.

Environmental Pollution

Pollution is the introduction of contaminants into the natural environment that cause adverse changes. These contaminants can take many forms, such as solid, liquid, or gaseous substances, or even energy in the form of radioactivity, heat, sound, or light. Pollution can be caused by natural events, but it is often linked to human activities like manufacturing, agriculture, and waste management. Pollution affects both human health and the environment, leading to widespread consequences.

Human Impact on the Environment

Human activities such as overpopulation, pollution, deforestation, and the burning of fossil fuels have significantly impacted the physical environment. These actions have triggered climate change, soil erosion, poor air quality, and contaminated water supplies, all of which threaten ecosystems and human life. Addressing these challenges requires global cooperation and sustainable practices to protect the environment for future generations.

Nature Conservation

Conservation efforts focus on protecting habitats, preventing deforestation, maintaining soil health, halting species extinction, reducing overfishing, and mitigating climate change. These goals are driven by the belief that the natural world has intrinsic and utilitarian value, and that preserving it is essential for the well-being of future generations. Conservationists advocate for responsible use of resources, the protection of ecosystems, and the prevention of environmental harm.
